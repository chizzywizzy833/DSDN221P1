Redesigning the Nintendo Switch Joy-Con for Repairability

A sustainability-focused redesign addressing joystick drift, repair accessibility, and electronic waste.

By Chisomo Kamphambe

Project Focus

  • Product: Nintendo Switch Joy-Con
  • Brand: Nintendo
  • Type: Wireless game controller
  • Design Aim: Repairability + lifespan extension
01

Life Cycle Research

The Nintendo Switch Joy-Con is a compact wireless controller designed for portability and flexibility. However, when examined through a life cycle perspective, several sustainability issues emerge across its material, manufacturing, use, and end-of-life stages.

In the raw materials stage, Joy-Cons rely on plastics, metals, and electronic components, including lithium-ion batteries and circuit boards. The extraction and processing of these materials contribute to environmental degradation, resource depletion, and energy consumption (Global E-waste Monitor, 2024). These impacts occur before the product reaches the user and are often overlooked.

During manufacturing and assembly, the Joy-Con is designed to be lightweight and compact, prioritising portability and cost efficiency. This results in tightly integrated internal components that are not optimised for accessibility or repair. While Nintendo outlines environmental considerations in product design, teardown and repair data from iFixit demonstrates that the internal layout remains difficult to access and repair (iFixit, n.d.). These design decisions directly influence the product’s lifespan.

In the distribution stage, Joy-Cons are manufactured and transported globally, contributing to environmental impacts through packaging and freight. These impacts increase when users are required to purchase replacement controllers due to faults.

The most significant sustainability concerns occur during the use phase. A widely reported issue known as Joy-Con drift is caused by wear within the joystick mechanism, leading to inaccurate input over time (iFixit, 2023). Although individual components can be replaced, repair requires disassembly, specialised tools, and technical skill. This creates a barrier for many users and often results in replacement instead of repair. The scale of this issue is reflected in regulatory attention, with the European Commission requiring Nintendo to provide free repairs for affected controllers (European Commission, 2023).

At the end-of-life stage, this contributes to electronic waste. Global e-waste reached 62 million tonnes in 2022, with only 22.3% formally collected and recycled (Global E-waste Monitor, 2024). Products like the Joy-Con, which are difficult to repair and relatively inexpensive to replace, are particularly prone to premature disposal.

Overall, the Joy-Con demonstrates how design decisions prioritising compactness and cost can reduce repairability, shorten product lifespan, and contribute to unnecessary environmental impact.

02

Proposed Redesign

Redesign Statement

This redesign addresses two key sustainability issues in the Nintendo Switch Joy-Con: premature joystick failure and limited repair accessibility. While the current design allows internal components to be replaced, the process requires full disassembly and technical skill, discouraging repair and promoting replacement.

The proposed redesign introduces an accessible internal repair system, focused on high-wear components. The joystick is redesigned as a removable cartridge module, allowing it to be replaced without dismantling the entire controller. In addition, the back panel is redesigned to provide direct and safe access to key components such as the joystick and battery, using standard fasteners rather than specialised screws.

Rather than applying full modularity, this approach targets the most common failure points while maintaining the controller’s compact form. The internal layout is simplified to reduce the risk of damage during repair and to improve usability.

By extending the usable lifespan of the Joy-Con and reducing the need for full replacement, this redesign reduces electronic waste and promotes a shift from disposable to repairable consumer electronics. It also changes the relationship between users and the product, encouraging active participation in maintenance and supporting longer-term ownership.

Key Impacts Addressed

Premature Failure

Joystick drift shortens product lifespan and leads to avoidable replacement.

Repair Barriers

Current repairs require full disassembly, specialised tools, and technical confidence.

03

Repair Interface

• Replaceable joystick cartridge

• Accessible battery compartment

• Back panel designed for easy repair access

• No full disassembly required for key repairs

Joystick Module

Issue: Drift detected

The joystick is redesigned as a removable cartridge module so it can be replaced without dismantling the full controller.

Repair Steps

  1. Open the back access panel.
  2. Remove the worn joystick module.
  3. Insert the replacement cartridge.
  4. Close and secure the panel.

Benefit: Reduces waste and extends product lifespan.

Battery Access

Issue: Reduced battery capacity over time

The redesigned back panel provides safer, more direct access to the battery, allowing replacement without risky internal disassembly.

Repair Steps

  1. Open the back access panel.
  2. Disconnect the battery safely.
  3. Insert a new battery module.
  4. Close and fasten the panel.

Benefit: Supports longer-term ownership and maintenance.

Back Panel Redesign

Issue: Difficult internal access

Instead of specialised screws and full teardown, the redesign uses standard fasteners and a more repair-friendly access path.

Design Changes

  1. Use standard screw heads.
  2. Create direct access to high-wear components.
  3. Simplify internal layout near the access area.
  4. Reduce repair risk for ordinary users.

Benefit: Makes repair more practical and less intimidating.
